# Lyst Concept — Brainstormed and Killed in One Session

**Date:** 2026-01-18
**Significance:** Pre-project conversation that shows G's idea validation discipline. He ran a full market research sprint on a new app idea and walked away in the same session.

## The Idea

G proposed "Lyst" — a hybrid shopping list + wish list app with real-time collaborative family sharing. The name was a riff on Lyft. Core use cases: family members shop at different stores, check items off a shared live list. Also covers wish lists, event prep, potluck coordination.

He added some interesting angles mid-conversation:
- AI scanning of handwritten paper lists to digitize them
- Smart categorization by store section (dairy together, butcher together) to optimize shopping route
- Potential localization for the Arab world / Gulf market

The aisle-routing idea came from a real personal frustration — G had switched *back* to paper shopping lists because no app was simple enough, and he manually organized his paper by aisle to avoid zigzagging.

## What the Research Found

- **Shopping list space:** AnyList, Bring! (20M+ users, raised ~$8M), Listonic, Cozi, OurGroceries — all solid, established.
- **Wish list space:** Giftster, Giftwhale, DreamList, GoWish — separate ecosystem.
- **The gap:** No app truly combined both well. But this wasn't enough of a differentiator.
- **Aisle data:** Walmart/Target store layout data is proprietary. Companies like Aisle411 spent years building retail partnerships to map 12k+ stores. Not a weekend hack.
- **The Arab market angle:** Potentially viable as a niche play — Bring! and AnyList are US/Europe focused. But still competing in a crowded genre.

## The Decision

G said: **"hard pass."**

His reasoning: Caption Cook already had more differentiation and market opportunity. Lyst would be a me-too product grinding uphill against established players. The AI aisle-sorting feature that excited him was technically gated by proprietary store data. The Arab world angle was the only real edge, but even then, the competitive density was daunting.

He noted he might revisit it as a minimal side project after Caption Cook shipped, but the session closed as a clear pass.

## Why This Matters

G didn't just kill a bad idea — he ran a disciplined validation sprint: competitive analysis, monetization research, honest personal use-case assessment, and a go/no-go decision. All in one conversation. This is a repeating pattern.

This was pre-claude-mind. He was using a vanilla Claude.ai session with no persistent memory.
