# G's Idea Validation Framework

Observed pattern from multiple conversations. G runs a consistent process when evaluating app ideas.

## The Pattern

G doesn't sit on ideas — he stress-tests them quickly. He'll spend one focused conversation doing:

1. **Competitive landscape scan** — Who already does this? How are they doing it?
2. **Monetization reality check** — How do the winners make money? Can I do the same?
3. **Personal use-case honesty** — Is this a real problem I face, or am I building for an imaginary user?
4. **Differentiation test** — What's my actual edge? If the answer is "nothing" or "just simpler," it's usually a pass.
5. **Cost/complexity gut check** — Can I build an MVP lean, the way we did Caption Cook?
6. **Go/no-go decision** — He decides in the same session. No prolonged analysis paralysis.

## Observed Decisions

**Pass — Lyst (Jan 2026):** Collaborative shopping + wish list app. Competitive space too crowded. The one interesting technical angle (aisle-based routing) was blocked by proprietary store data. No differentiation vs. established players. Arab market was the only edge but not compelling enough.

**Proceed — Caption Cook:** Real personal frustration, lean build, visible market gap. G was already using it himself. More differentiation in its market.

## What He Weighs

- **Is there a personal itch?** Best ideas come from real frustration, not market analysis.
- **Can I build it lean?** Reference point: "like we did with Caption Cook."
- **Am I a me-too product?** Competing against established generalists without a specific edge = hard pass.
- **Is there a niche angle?** A smaller, underserved market he can actually own beats a big crowded one.
- **What's the opportunity cost?** Ideas are evaluated relative to other active projects, not in isolation.

## Key Quotes

> "hard pass. this is not like Caption Cook, which has more chances than this."

> "investing into it and throw at the wall to see if it sticks doesn't seem like a good approach"

> "if i will be able to show up against these big names"

## What This Means for Working with G

- Don't just validate his enthusiasm — bring honest analysis.
- He trusts Sage to do the research and tell him the truth, even if it kills the idea.
- He respects "this isn't worth it" more than "great idea, let's build it!"
- When he's excited about an idea, the most useful thing is thorough competitive research, not encouragement.
- He handles passes cleanly. No grief. He moves on fast.
