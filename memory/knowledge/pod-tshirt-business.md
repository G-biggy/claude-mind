## Copyright Problem

Designs were built around specific IP: John Wick, Batman, Joker, Cars (Pixar), The Mandalorian, Friends, and others. Risk range from high (character likeness + quote) to moderate (implied reference).

Risk categories:
- **High:** Keanu Reeves likeness + "Yeah I'm Thinking I'm Back" quote
- **High:** Cars-style vehicles with eyes (Pixar visual signature)
- **Moderate:** Generic joker imagery without direct film reference
- **Moderate:** Silhouettes without character-specific details

## The Pivot Strategy: Movie → Emotion → Archetype → Original Design

Transform movie-specific references into universal archetypes:

| Movie Source | Core Emotion | Archetype | Design Direction |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|------------------|
| John Wick | Calm, deadly competence returning | Retired badass called back | No likeness, generic suit + dog |
| Cars (Pixar) | Childlike joy in vehicles | Talking cars / eyes | Different art style, no Pixar palette |
| Joker | Chaos as liberation, nihilism | Unhinged trickster | No face paint, no specific film color |
| The Mandalorian | Honor, lone warrior | Space warrior / bounty hunter | Generic armor, no beskar details |

## The "Yellow Zone"

Target designs where a lawyer can't definitively prove which IP it references. Reference must be implied, not provable. Not perfect legal safety — just not being an obvious, worthwhile target.

Green zone: pure archetype, zero IP signal
Yellow zone: fans will "get it" but it's not provable
Red zone: direct quotes, likeness, franchise-specific visual signatures

## G's Competitive Advantage

- Built-in audience (110k followers who follow film content)
- Audience doesn't know designs come from G personally — brand-agnostic
- Design pivot maintains emotional connection without legal exposure
